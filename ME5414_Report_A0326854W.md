## 1. Introduction and Algorithms

This report documents the numerical evaluation and practical comparison of two selected algorithms for solving Linear Programming (LP) problems. In compliance with the guidelines for Group B (even matriculation digit), the computational performance of a non-interior point method, specifically the **Revised Simplex Algorithm**, is compared against an **Interior-Point Method (IPM)**. 

To conduct a fair and efficient comparison, the highly optimized HiGHS solvers integrated within SciPy (`method='highs-ds'` for Dual Simplex and `method='highs-ipm'` for IPM) were utilized.

### 1.1 Non-Interior Point Method: Revised Simplex Algorithm
The Simplex algorithm traverses the edges of the feasible convex polytope, moving from one basic feasible solution (vertex) to adjacent ones, strictly improving (or maintaining) the objective function. The standard simplex method maintains a full tableau which is computationally expensive for large problems. The revised simplex method improves upon this by only maintaining and updating the inverse of the basis matrix ($B^{-1}$), reducing per-iteration computational effort and memory overhead. While its worst-case complexity is exponential, it demonstrates remarkable efficiency in practical problems, especially when exploiting problem sparsity.

### 1.2 Interior-Point Method
In contrast to the edge-walking behavior of the simplex method, Interior-Point methods traverse the interior of the feasible region. By employing barrier functions (typically logarithmic) to prevent the solution from hitting the inequality boundaries prematurely, the problem is formulated as a sequence of unconstrained optimization problems. Using Newton-like steps, IPM drives the solution towards the optimal vertex. IPM is theoretically guaranteed to run in polynomial time ($O(n^{3.5}L)$) and is particularly advantageous for large-scale, dense LP problems where the number of constraints causes simplex methods to stall.

## 2. Methodology and Test Problem Generation

The performance evaluation is conducted using randomly generated LP problems of the standard form:
$$ \min c^T x \quad \text{s.t.} \quad Ax \leq b, \quad x \geq 0 $$

To ensure valid, bounded, and feasible test problems across all scales:
1. **Initial Feasible Point Verification:** A point $x_0 \in (0, 10)$ is randomly generated. The RHS constraint bounds are then artificially enforced as $b = Ax_0 + \text{slack}$, where $\text{slack} > 0$. This guarantees the existence of a strictly feasible interior point. No phase-I artificial variable initialization is required.
2. **Boundedness:** To prevent the solver from returning an unbounded status (which skews computational time), explicit upper bounds ($x_j \leq 100 \ \forall j$) are appended to the inequality matrix $A$.
3. **Evaluation Metrics:** The algorithms are compared on elapsed computational time (CPU wall-clock time in seconds) and the number of solver iterations.

## 3. Computational Findings

Three core experiments were conducted by keeping the stopping tolerance at $10^{-8}$ (unless otherwise specified). The runtime measurements are averaged over 5 independent trials per configuration.

### 3.1 Varying Decision Variables ($n$) with Fixed Constraints
The number of inequalities was anchored at $m=100$, while the dimension of decision variables ($n$) swelled from 50 to 1000. 
* **Observations:** Both methods exhibit increased computational times as dimensions grow. Across the spectrum, the Revised Simplex completed the optimization faster (scaling up to 0.027s at $n=1000$) compared to the Interior-Point method (0.055s at $n=1000$). 
* **Iterations:** Predictably, the Simplex algorithm required significantly more iterations (scaling from 90 to 198 iterations). In stark contrast, IPM converged in a highly stable manner, resting persistently between 11 and 16 iterations regardless of problem size. This validates the theoretical trait of IPMs: high cost per iteration, but very few required iterations to converge.

### 3.2 Varying Constraints ($m$) with Fixed Decision Variables
The variable dimension was fixed at $n=100$, scaling constraints ($m$) from 50 to 1000.
* **Observations:** As the complexity of the constraint polytope increased, both solvers experienced non-linear runtime inflation. Simplex times ranged from 0.001s to 0.064s, whereas IPM tracked closely from 0.003s to 0.078s. 
* **Iterations:** The Simplex method's iterations skyrocketed from 64 to over 432, revealing its sensitivity to the number of hyperplanes it must navigate. True to its design, IPM bypassed this issue, maintaining a near-constant span of 13 to 15 iterations even when the problem was densely constrained.

### 3.3 Sensitivity to Stopping Tolerance
With a fixed medium-scale problem ($n=200, m=200$), the dual/primal feasibility tolerances were tightened successively from $10^{-4}$ to $10^{-10}$.
* **Observations:** The internal iteration count for both algorithms demonstrated absolute invariability (Simplex locked at 327 iterations; IPM at 17 iterations). Consequently, the CPU time remained relatively insensitive to the tolerance scaling, registering flat computational times of ~0.019s for Simplex and ~0.067s for IPM. This implies that for the scale of test problems evaluated, both the optimal basis identification (Simplex) and the barrier limit (IPM) are achieved with high numerical stability right away, rendering the stopping parameters largely superficial once the optimal active set is identified. 

## 4. Conclusion
The experimental results align well with theoretical expectations. For the moderately sized LP constructs in this evaluation, the **Revised Simplex Algorithm** is computationally superior in terms of raw CPU tracking. However, its efficiency degrades rapidly with increased constraints due to the exploding vertex-to-vertex pathing requirements. The **Interior-Point Method**, while burdened by a heavier algebraic matrix inversion overhead per iteration, demonstrates an impressive path-following stability, making it practically invariant to constraint complexity. Consequently, while Simplex forms the preferable baseline for sparse or smaller geometries, IPM operates as the robust choice for massively scaled dense environments.
